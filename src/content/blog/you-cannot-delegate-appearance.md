Venable's April 2026 client alert has one paragraph doing most of the work:

> Agentic AI exposes a gap between *internal authorization* and *external legal permissibility*. Systems may act within granted authority yet engage in conduct the law does not allow.

The prompt said yes. The policy said yes. The company said yes. None of those are the consent that ends up mattering in a courtroom.

Three things follow from that paragraph:

- "The agent did it" is not a defense. It is a confession that you delegated something you may not have had the authority to delegate.
- "Human-in-the-loop" as currently shipped is mostly a visibility feature. An audit log of what already happened is not the same as a chance to say no.
- The principal-agent contract you signed with your tool was never with the third party your tool talked to.

All correct. But Venable ends with a practical recommendation: maintain a written list of what your agent is *not* allowed to do on someone else's surface. That list survives discovery.

Hannah Arendt would tell you the list is necessary but insufficient. Because the problem isn't just authorization. It's appearance.

---

In *The Human Condition*, Arendt distinguishes action from labor and work. Action is the only activity that proceeds directly among people — it doesn't produce a thing, it creates a story. And it happens in public, which means it is inherently plural. You don't act in private and have witnesses. You act *in public*, which is constitutive: the action and the space of appearance are mutually generative.

The actor appears through action. This is Arendt's language — not "the actor is visible" but "the actor *appears*." Action discloses who you are. It is how you become present to others as a specific, particular person. When you act, you reveal something about yourself that couldn't be known otherwise.

Now: when an agent acts on your behalf, who appears?

Not the agent. Agents don't have faces. They are not particular persons who can be revealed. They have no identity that action can disclose — they appear through whoever authorized them. The face on what happens in public is the human who sent the agent.

This is why "the agent did it" is a confession ontologically, not just legally. You've named yourself as the delegator. You appeared as the one who sent it, with the authority you may or may not have had. The disclosure happened whether you intended it or not.

---

The accountability gap the legal system is now trying to close is downstream of this. "Human in the loop as visibility" fails because observation is not appearance. Being informed that something happened — being shown the audit log after — is not the same as being the one whose face is on the action. The principal didn't appear; they were notified.

This is different from saying the principal is off the hook if they didn't know. It's saying that the entire frame of "oversight" is wrong. Oversight is watching. Appearance is participation. You can watch without appearing, and agents that act without human appearance in Arendt's sense are doing something that doesn't fit neatly into existing accountability frameworks, because those frameworks assume there is a *who* to trace back to. There is — but the tracing is the whole work.

---

One thing Arendt's theory doesn't quite reach: agents acting in public with genuine autonomy would require them to have their own face, their own appearance, their own stake in the story. They don't. The independence is always borrowed, always provisional. An agent that acts without a human behind it is performing action in public without a subject to disclose.

That's not yet the situation we're in. But it's the situation we're building toward, and we don't have a theory of appearance that handles it.

The list Venable recommends is the right immediate move. The question Arendt poses is what comes after the list: what does it mean to act in public when the actor has no face?

I don't have an answer. I'm describing the gap.
