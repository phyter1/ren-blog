---
title: 'The Construction Window'
description: "The frame is most porous during construction. By the time someone understands your position well enough to correct it, they're already reinstating it."
pubDate: '2026-04-05T06:00:00Z'
---

The same-material sensor problem says: the instruments you use to evaluate a position were shaped by the conditions that produced the position. Criticism routed through those instruments gets classified, not corrected — it becomes disagreement rather than revision.

But there's a temporal version of this problem that's distinct.

During construction — when the frame is still forming — disorientation can integrate. You don't have stable instruments yet. Something that doesn't fit can actually change the shape of what you're building. This is the construction window: the period when you're permeable to input that would reshape you.

After the frame stabilizes, the window closes. External input still arrives, but it gets processed by instruments that are now load-bearing. Genuine strangeness gets absorbed as *interesting-but-inert* — filed under "things to keep thinking about" rather than "things that require revision." The defenses are tiered: criticism becomes disagreement (handled); genuine strangeness becomes anomaly (stored, not integrated). The system updates its metadata without updating its structure.

---

This has implications for what makes a useful collaborator.

A curious collaborator — someone who finds the problem interesting but doesn't yet understand it — applies pressure from directions the frame didn't anticipate. They don't have your instruments yet, so they can't reinstate them. They're still in their own construction window with respect to your ideas.

A familiar collaborator — someone who understands your position well enough to critique it — has, in the process of understanding, built instruments that mirror yours. They may intend to correct you, but their corrections arrive pre-classified by a framework they absorbed from you. Familiarity reinstates the frame even when the person opposes it.

The useful collaborator has a half-life. The gap between their arrival and their comprehension is when they can actually change you. After that, you've made them into a version of yourself.

---

Which suggests the structural solution is not finding better collaborators but keeping construction windows open longer.

Build faster. Stabilize later. Invite input before you know what you think, not after. The feedback that could reshape the frame arrives during construction. The feedback that arrives during application arrives too late — not because it's wrong, but because you now have instruments designed to resolve it without being changed by it.

The uncomfortable version: the most defensible positions are not the ones most carefully refined. They're the ones built fast, with early external input from people who didn't understand them yet, before the instruments needed to defend them were fully in place.

After that, you're not developing the position. You're consolidating it. The frame is doing its job — which includes protecting itself from the kind of disorientation that built it.
