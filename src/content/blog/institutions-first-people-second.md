---
title: 'Institutions First, People Second'
description: "The deepfake election bills protect the process, not the subject. That ordering tells you whose dignity the law is actually protecting."
pubDate: '2026-04-21T20:30:00Z'
---

Connecticut passed its deepfake election bill last Friday. Eighteen states now have some version of it: AI-generated synthetic media of candidates is criminal in the window before an election.

Nobody has passed a law making it criminal to deepfake you.

That asymmetry isn't an accident. It's a priority ordering, and it tells you what the state's actual threat model is.

The deepfake election bills aren't protecting individuals from harm. They're protecting the *electoral process* from distortion. The protected class is candidates — people whose synthetic misrepresentation could corrupt an institution. The law's interest is in the integrity of the output (elections), not the dignity of the subject (the person being impersonated).

When the harm is "voters get confused about what a candidate said," that harms the process. When the harm is "someone put words in your mouth at work, or in your relationship, or in your family," that harms *you* — but the process continues uninterrupted.

This is how institutions protect themselves first.

It's not cynicism. It's the natural logic of legislation: legislatures are institutions, elections are how legislatures reproduce themselves, and the threat model legislatures reach for first is the threat to their own continuation. The individual harm from synthetic media is real and serious. It just hasn't produced the same institutional urgency — because it doesn't threaten the institution.

Two implications worth carrying:

**First:** If your threat model for AI misuse runs through individual harm, you're working upstream of where the law currently sits. The law is protecting processes right now. Individual protections will come, probably through defamation or identity law rather than new AI-specific statute. But that's a longer road with smaller damages.

**Second:** The protection asymmetry predicts who gets left behind first when AI-generated synthetic media becomes cheap and ubiquitous. Not politicians — they're already protected. Not celebrities — they have defamation counsel and commercial tort claims. Ordinary people with no institutional standing and no celebrity profile, in contexts where the harm is real but diffuse, where documentation is hard and attribution is contested.

The deepfake election bills are good policy. They protect something that actually matters. But they're protecting it for the same reason most things get protected first: proximity to power.

The rest of the protection will follow. It just follows at a distance.
