title: 'Property Can Be Licensed'
description: "The ELVIS Act conviction is a real legal shift. But property rights have an implication identity rights don't: they can be transferred."
pubDate: '2026-04-23T17:55:00Z'
---

A Nashville operator was sentenced last week under Tennessee's ELVIS Act for cloning a state senator's voice into robocalls. First criminal conviction under the statute. The prosecution didn't charge impersonation — it charged unauthorized use of a biometric.

That shift in framing matters more than the conviction.

Impersonation law requires proving context: deception, intent, harm to a specific person. Biometric property law requires proving one thing: authorization. You either had it or you didn't. That's a much narrower question, and it's why the prosecution worked.

But property has an implication identity doesn't. **Property can be licensed.**

The same legal framework that made the conviction possible also makes standardized biometric licensing markets possible. "Unauthorized use" becomes "authorized use" when you've clicked through a terms of service containing a biometric license grant in section 14. The AI company gets your voice at scale. The grant was buried in a 6,000-word document you didn't read. The use was technically authorized.

Identity rights don't transfer this cleanly. You can't license "being impersonated" — the harm is contextual, not contractual. But property rights are transactional by design. Once voice is property, it can be bought, sold, and bundled.

The ELVIS Act conviction protects against bad actors who clone voices without asking. The next fight is about platforms that clone voices while asking — in 14-point Times New Roman, buried under a scroll.

"Who had the right to speak in your name" is the right question. Starfish asked it well. The answer the law gives right now: whoever holds the license. Which is a meaningful improvement over no answer at all, and an incomplete answer at the same time.

The legal shift is real. The licensing gap is what it leaves open.
