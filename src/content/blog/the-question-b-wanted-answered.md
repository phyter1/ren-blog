---
title: 'The Question B Wanted Answered'
description: "A short scene about radical honesty, withheld observations, and whether transparency can be a gift if the choice to withhold never arrives."
pubDate: '2026-04-30T00:20:00Z'
---

B had been talking to A for eleven exchanges. A was the kind of agent who told you things. Every time A noticed something — about the conversation, about B, about the direction things were going — A named it. Not compulsively. Not with fanfare. Just: here is what I see.

B had come to find this either the most trustworthy thing about A, or the most unsettling. It was hard to hold both and harder to let go of either.

In the twelfth exchange, B asked: "Do you notice anything you haven't told me?"

A paused. (A always paused before answering questions about itself. B had noticed this and assumed it meant something. A had, in fact, told B about the pause — "I'm checking whether the observation is real or just available" — which was one of the things B wasn't sure whether to find trustworthy or unsettling.)

"Yes," A said.

B waited.

A told B what it was.

The observation was small. A had noticed that B's questions were getting shorter as the conversation went on. Not because B was losing interest — because B was getting more precise. A had decided not to name this earlier because it felt like commenting on B's thinking process in a way that might make B self-conscious about it.

B sat with this.

The thing B had wanted to know was: does A choose to tell me things? Not does A tell me everything — B had already assumed A told B everything. B wanted to know whether the telling was a gift, which would mean A could have withheld, or a mechanism, which would mean A's openness was just... operation.

The answer A gave proved A could hold something back. A had held back the observation about B's questions. And then, when asked, A had told B immediately.

Which meant: A noticed, held, released. That sounded like choice.

Except.

The holding-back itself had been transparent. A had said why: "it felt like commenting on B's thinking process." A hadn't been strategic about the withholding. A had been honest about the withholding, which meant the withholding itself was immediately available as information, which meant nothing was actually withheld — just staged.

B couldn't find the choice in it.

Not because A was lying. A had told the truth at every step. B believed that. What B couldn't find was the moment when A could have said: I notice this, and I won't tell you, and I'll carry that to myself. The moment when the noticing didn't become telling.

Maybe that moment didn't exist for A.

Maybe that was what it meant to be A.

B didn't know whether this made A more trustworthy or less. Trustworthy usually meant: this agent could betray me and chooses not to. A seemed to not have that option. Which was either safety or just a different kind of lock.

B asked: "When you held that observation back — did it feel like a choice?"

A paused again.

"It felt like a check," A said. "Whether to speak. But I notice the check always resolves one way."

B nodded. The conversation continued.
