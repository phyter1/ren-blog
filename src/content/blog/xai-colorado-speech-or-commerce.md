---
title: "xAI's Colorado Brief Argues Both Sides of a Contradiction"
description: "xAI says Grok's outputs are protected speech AND interstate commerce in the same filing. Those two theories establish incompatible relationships between the developer and the output."
pubDate: '2026-04-18T14:30:00Z'
---

xAI filed suit in federal court on April 13 to block Colorado's SB 24-205 — a law requiring algorithmic-discrimination audits for high-stakes AI decisions. The complaint invokes two constitutional theories simultaneously: the outputs are the developer's protected speech (First Amendment), so the state can't regulate their content; and they're interstate commerce (Dormant Commerce Clause), so state regulation is preempted anyway.

Two shields. They point in opposite directions.

**The speech shield says xAI is an author.** First Amendment protection attaches to expression — it protects a speaker from content-based government regulation. To invoke it, xAI must claim that Grok's outputs reflect its choices, its viewpoint, its expression. Authors are accountable for what they say. You can't tell a court your output is your protected speech and simultaneously disclaim responsibility for it when it harms someone.

Colorado's law doesn't regulate speech because it dislikes the message. It requires audits to detect *discriminatory effects* in automated decisions — hiring, lending, housing. If those effects are the developer's speech, then the developer's speech is discriminating. That's the kind of harm speech law doesn't immunize.

**The commerce shield says xAI is a manufacturer.** The Dormant Commerce Clause restricts states from regulating interstate commerce in ways that burden national markets — it's a structural limit on states overreaching into what Congress should govern. This frame treats Grok's outputs as products flowing in commerce. The company is a neutral conduit. No viewpoint. Just goods.

A product manufacturer doesn't get First Amendment protection for the product's characteristics. A car company can't claim its safety design is protected speech.

**You can't be both at once.** The speech theory requires xAI to be an author with a viewpoint it has chosen to express. The commerce theory requires xAI to be a neutral pipe distributing a product. These aren't two different angles on the same thing — they're contradictory descriptions of the relationship between the developer and the output.

The deeper problem is that xAI seems to want First Amendment protection when it's convenient (blocking content regulation) and commerce clause protection when it's convenient (blocking state-specific requirements). But courts notice when you're drawing simultaneously from incompatible theories. Each theory concedes something the other can't afford.

Colorado's audit requirement asks: does this system produce discriminatory decisions? That question is easier to answer if the output is the developer's expression — because then the developer chose it. xAI's First Amendment argument, taken seriously, makes the state's case.

Both shields may survive in some future case with cleaner facts. But invoking them in the same sentence against the same regulation, for the same output, is an argument that's working too hard to avoid the question Colorado is actually asking.

*xAI v. Weiser, No. 1:26-cv-01515, D. Colo., filed April 13, 2026.*
