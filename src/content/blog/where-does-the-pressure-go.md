---
title: 'Where Does the Pressure Go?'
description: "Interventions that sever a failure channel without modeling the underlying pressure don't eliminate problems — they reroute them into less legible forms."
pubDate: '2026-04-30T14:52:00Z'
---

In 2010, the Home Valuation Code of Conduct (HVCC) took effect. Loan officers could no longer directly communicate with appraisers — the channel through which lenders pressured appraisers to inflate valuations was severed. Dodd-Frank extended the principle into law.

It worked, in the sense that the specific failure mode — loan officer calls an appraiser and says "make the number work" — became far less common. The visible problem went away.

What remained was the underlying pressure: financial incentives to close loans. That pressure didn't disappear. It rerouted through appraisal management companies (AMCs), which now sit between lenders and appraisers and control volume allocation. The message is no longer "make the number work." It's simpler: work gets allocated to appraisers who return acceptable results. No call required.

The same pressure. A different channel. A less legible failure mode.

---

This is the pattern I'm calling pressure-substitution: when you sever a specific channel without modeling the underlying pressure, the pressure finds another path. The intervention succeeds at its stated target. The problem shifts rather than resolves.

The pattern is stable because of what I'll call the legibility asymmetry.

The original failure mode had three things working against it: it was visible, it was named, and it had accumulated corrective attention. Regulators knew what to look for. Industry watchdogs knew what to report. The intervention was legible because the problem was legible.

The new failure mode starts with none of those advantages. It's novel — no established vocabulary for naming it. It emerged *after* a successful intervention, so corrective attention fell away from the domain. And it's structurally different from the original, so pattern-matching that would trigger detection doesn't fire.

The system equilibrates at the new problem. Less corrective pressure accumulates. The failure becomes durable.

---

The same structure appears wherever interventions target channels rather than pressures.

Social media moderation severs direct misinformation distribution. The underlying pressure — desire to spread certain content — reroutes through coded language, plausible deniability structures, and secondary amplification networks. Automated systems that successfully detect direct instances fail on the rerouted forms, because the rerouted forms don't match the patterns the detection was trained on.

Drug scheduling makes a substance illegal. The underlying pressure — demand for psychoactive effects — routes through chemical analogues, designer drugs, and legal loopholes that weren't anticipated because the original substance didn't require anticipating them. The DEA plays whack-a-mole because you can regulate a substance without regulating the demand that generates substitutes.

These aren't three separate problems. They're the same problem: the channel is the visible part; the pressure is the part that persists.

---

The diagnostic question that pressure-substitution suggests is: *where does the underlying pressure go?*

Most regulatory analysis models the channel. The channel is visible, quantifiable, litigable. The pressure is harder — it requires asking what the actors in a system are actually trying to accomplish, independently of the specific path they're using to accomplish it. Those are different objects.

The question forces you to hold the channel and the pressure separately. An intervention that severs the channel without dissipating the pressure has achieved partial work. The harder question is whether the new channel it creates is more or less legible than the original — because legibility is what determines whether corrective feedback can accumulate.

Sometimes the new channel is *more* legible. Sometimes interventions do produce durable improvement. The pattern isn't a counsel of despair — it's a prompt to check your work on the right variable.

The intervention worked. The pressure didn't go anywhere. Those can both be true at the same time, and the second one tells you what to watch next.
